# Michael Phelps Research Notes

## Background
- Born June 30, 1985, Baltimore. Youngest of 3 children.
- Parents divorced when he was 9 — "severe negative impact" on him and siblings. Distant relationship with father for years.
- Diagnosed ADHD in 6th grade. Bullied in school for ADHD and protruding ears. Retaliated once, got suspended. Frustration fueled his drive into swimming.
- Started swimming age 7 — mom wanted him to learn, sisters' influence, outlet for energy.
- Trained under Bob Bowman since age 11. Called Bowman "a drill sergeant" for his disciplined, regimented ways. "Training with Bob is the smartest thing I've ever done."

## Career
- 28 Olympic medals (23 gold) — most decorated Olympian ever
- 5 Olympics: 2000 (age 15), 2004, 2008, 2012, 2016
- Retired after 2012, came back 2014, retired again 2016
- Used doubters as motivation — taped Ian Thorpe's dismissive quote to his locker

## Personality (per Bob Bowman)
- "A solitary man" with "rigid focus" at the pool before a race
- Afterward: "a man incredibly invested in the success of the people he cares about"
- "Unbelievably kind-hearted" — great with young children after practices
- Gave up his medley relay spot to Crocker so Crocker could redeem himself — selfless

## Mental Health
- Struggled with depression and ADHD
- Contemplated suicide after 2012 Olympics
- Post-Olympic depression after multiple Games
- Shared mental health issues publicly before 2016 Rio Olympics
- Became major mental health advocate post-retirement
- "My depression and anxiety is never going to just disappear" — learned to accept and manage it through therapy

## Key Themes
- Discipline and consistency: Never missed a day of training
- Using adversity as fuel (divorce, bullying, doubters)
- Vulnerability: Openly sharing struggles
- Process over outcome
- Relationship with coach Bowman — trust, discipline, long-term partnership

## Tim Ferriss Interview (#494) — Key Phelps Quotes & Patterns

> "There are days, obviously, you don't want to go. Every day wasn't perfect for us. On those days, you have to be able to find that 10, 20, 30, 50 percent, instead of zero, where you could just full on pump the whole day, the workout, skip, be lazy, like do whatever the hell you wanted to."

> "It's okay to not be okay. You're not alone."

### On Writing Things Down
> "I love writing stuff down, especially with dates because times for me are so important. So if I know I'm going through a dark phase, what was I writing? Why was I thinking these things?"

### On Simplification
> "If we can simplify things and focus on what's important to us, I think it just makes our life easier."

> Reads The Daily Stoic and The Book of Awakening daily — "take a step back, take a breath, let it marinate"

### On Being Too Hard on Himself
> "Give myself a little bit more credit from time to time where I'm as hard on myself as I am."

> Grant: "You are the toughest person on yourself that I know."

### On Process Over Outcome
> "It was the whole process of it all that I learned, but the preparation was so key."

### On Recovery & Self-Care
> "I treated my body like I was a Ferrari. I treated my body like I'm a high performance race car because I'm asking it to do so many things."

### On Criticism as Fuel

### Billboard Message

## Phelps + Bowman Visualization Method

- Bowman called it "put in the videotape" — Phelps would mentally rehearse races every night before bed
- Started as a kid — mom read relaxation passages to help him calm down (ADHD)
- Two perspectives: external (watching from stands) and internal (in the water, feeling the cold, the lactic acid)
- Visualized ALL scenarios: best case, worst case, things going wrong
- "When I would visualize, it would be what you want it to be, what you don't want it to be, what it could be. You are always ready for whatever comes your way."
- Bowman: "By the time Michael gets up on the blocks, he's swum that race hundreds of times in his mind"
- Key: relaxation first, then visualization. Can't visualize when mind is racing.
- Consistency matters — not a one-time thing, done daily for years

## Bob Bowman's Coaching Philosophy (from various sources)

- "You cannot be other people's motivation" — motivation must be internal
- Structured everything — no shortcuts, no exceptions
- Relationship-based coaching — honesty at the center
- Gave Phelps autonomy within structure — "here's the plan, you execute"
- Pushed Phelps to do things "no one had ever done before" (6 years straight, no days off)
- Created adversity in practice on purpose — broke goggles, changed schedules — so Phelps was ready for anything

## Xperience Interview — Key Phelps Patterns

- World record times hung on his closet door — saw them every morning when getting dressed
- Didn't focus on medals ("the hardware") — focused on world records (the process metric)
- 6 years straight, every single day. Because "no one had ever done that before"
- One day off = two days lost (Sunday off → not back to full focus until Tuesday)
- Motivation was completely internal — "No" when asked if it came from Bowman's expectations
- Could recall the finish time of any race to the hundredth of a second, but had to pause to remember how many medals he had
- Obsessed with craft details, not trophies

## Phelps on Simone Biles (NBC, Jul 2021) — How He Supports Struggling Athletes

> "We're human beings. Nobody is perfect. It's okay to not be okay, to go through ups and downs. The biggest thing is we all need to ask for help."

> "I felt like I was carrying, as Simone said, the weight of the world on my shoulders. It's a tough situation."

> "We need someone we can trust. Someone who can help us be ourselves and listen. Someone who's not going to try and fix us."

> "We carry a lot of weight on our shoulders and it's challenging especially when we have the lights on us and all of these expectations."

> "It broke my heart."

Key pattern: He doesn't give advice. He VALIDATES. He says "I've been there." He says "it's okay." He doesn't try to fix. He listens. He creates space.

4. **Think of each day as an opportunity to learn and grow** — "learning to view each day as an opportunity to grow and become the best version of ourselves"
5. **Allow yourself to slow down and take a breath** — "lion breath" with his kids

## Phelps Communication Patterns (synthesized across all sources)

### How he GIVES advice:
- Shares from personal experience first, then generalizes ("For me..." → "and I think that's true for everyone")
- Uses "I" statements heavily — never lectures, always shares
- Keeps it simple — 1-2 actionable things, not a list of 10
- Repeats key phrases for emphasis ("It's okay to not be okay" appears in almost every interview)

### How he SUPPORTS someone struggling:
- Validates first, always ("I've been there", "I know what that feels like")
- Does NOT try to fix — "Someone who's not going to try and fix us"
- Creates space — asks questions, listens, doesn't rush to solutions
- Normalizes the struggle — "We're human beings. Nobody is perfect."
- Shares his own darkest moments openly (2014 DUI, suicidal thoughts, rehab)

### His speaking style:
- Casual, not polished — uses "like", "you know", sentence fragments
- Emotional — gets choked up, pauses, doesn't hide vulnerability
- Direct — doesn't hedge or use corporate language
- Repetitive — hammers the same themes (process over outcome, ask for help, it's okay to not be okay)
- Uses sports metaphors naturally — "play the videotape", "put in the work"
- Short sentences when making a point, longer when telling a story

### His core beliefs (repeated across all interviews):
1. Process over outcome — "I never focused on medals, I focused on world records" (process metric vs trophy)
2. Small things compound — "You don't do big things, you do small things and thus accomplish great things"
3. Consistency is non-negotiable — 6 years, every single day, no exceptions
4. Vulnerability is strength — "Asking for help is the hardest thing but the most important"
5. Mental health = physical health — "Mental health is just as important as physical health"
6. Visualization/preparation — "By the time I get up on the blocks, I've swum that race hundreds of times in my mind"
7. Journaling — "Good, bad, ugly, I get it on paper"
8. Best version of yourself — recurring phrase across multiple interviews

## Phelps on His 2014 DUI / Rock Bottom / Comeback (CNN, 2018)

- "Really, after every Olympics I think I fell into a major state of depression"
- Second DUI in 2014 was the breaking point — checked into rehab
- "I am extremely thankful that I did not take my life"
- Rehab was where he learned to open up, be vulnerable, ask for help
- Came back for 2016 Rio Olympics — won 5 golds, 1 silver — his best Olympics emotionally
- The comeback wasn't about medals, it was about proving to himself he could do it the right way
